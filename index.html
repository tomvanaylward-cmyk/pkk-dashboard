About this dashboard

A guide for leadership and non-technical users on how to read and interpret the PKK pass probability predictions.

?
What is this dashboard?
This tool predicts the probability that a specific vehicle will pass its mandatory Norwegian vehicle inspection — the EU-kontroll (PKK). It is built on open data from Statens vegvesen and updates automatically every night with the latest inspection records.
%
What does the percentage mean?
A prediction of 72% means: out of 100 vehicles with identical brand, fuel type, age and mileage, approximately 72 would be expected to pass their PKK inspection. It is a statistical estimate — not a guarantee for any individual vehicle.
~
What does the confidence interval mean?
The range shown below the percentage (e.g. 68% – 76%) is the 95% confidence interval. This means we are 95% confident the true pass rate for this type of vehicle falls within that range. A narrow interval means high certainty. A wide interval means the model is less sure — treat wide-interval predictions as directional rather than precise.
#
What is the inspection number?
In Norway, vehicles must have their first PKK inspection 4 years after registration, then every 2 years. The dashboard automatically calculates which inspection number a vehicle is on based on its registration year. A vehicle registered in 2018 is now on its 3rd inspection (due 2026). First-time inspections pass at a much higher rate (~85%) than later ones (~65–70%).
!
Why is Tesla's pass rate low?
Tesla's negative coefficient in the model reflects the Norwegian fleet as a whole. Norway has one of the world's oldest Tesla fleets — many vehicles registered in 2013–2016 are now on their 5th or 6th inspection and have accumulated real faults (suspension, door seals, 12V battery, brake wear). Newer models (Model 3 from 2019+, Model Y from 2021+) likely perform better than the brand-wide figure suggests. The model cannot distinguish between models within a brand.
i
What does AUC-ROC 0.836 mean?
AUC-ROC measures how well the model separates vehicles that pass from those that fail. A score of 1.0 is perfect prediction. A score of 0.5 is no better than random guessing. Our score of 0.836 is considered strong for this type of data — roughly equivalent to saying the model correctly ranks a passing vehicle above a failing one 84% of the time.
~
What are the most common failure reasons?
Nationally, the three most common failure chapters are: Brakes (most frequent across all fuel types), Lights & electrical, and Axles, wheels & tyres. Electric vehicles rarely fail on emissions but still fail on brakes and lights. The full breakdown is shown in the "Most common failure chapters" section on the predictor page.
E
What is Etterkontroll (E)?
Etterkontroll is a re-inspection after a vehicle has previously failed. These vehicles have already had repairs done, so they pass at a much higher rate than standard Periodisk (P) inspections. Selecting Etterkontroll in the predictor will significantly increase the predicted pass probability — this is real and expected.
*
Important limitations
The model predicts for a type of vehicle — not a specific car. It does not know about individual service history, recent repairs, recalls, or software updates. Predictions for vehicles with very few examples in the data (rare brands, unusual fuel types) carry wider uncertainty. Do not use these predictions as a substitute for an actual mechanical inspection.
D
Data source & methodology
Data: Statens vegvesen — Periodisk Kjøretøykontroll (PKK), published quarterly on GitHub under CC BY 4.0. Model: logistic regression trained on ~480,000 inspection records. Confidence intervals: bootstrap resampling (8 resamples). Retrains automatically every night at 03:00 UTC via GitHub Actions. View source code →
